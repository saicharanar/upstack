You now know both libraries. The question learners always ask is "which one?" — and the honest
answer is *both have a home*. Here's how to decide quickly.

## Declarative vs imperative

- **Motion is declarative.** You describe states (`initial`/`animate`, variants) and it's deeply
  integrated with React's render and mount/unmount lifecycle. Reach for it for **component state
  transitions, gestures, layout animations, and exit animations**.
- **GSAP is imperative.** You command a timeline step by step. It's framework-agnostic and
  unmatched for **complex sequencing, fine-grained timing, and scroll-driven** work (ScrollTrigger).

## It's fine to use both

They don't conflict. A common, healthy split in one app:

- **Motion** handles the React-y things: page/route transitions, a modal's enter/exit, a list's
  staggered mount, hover states.
- **GSAP** handles a **hero timeline** or a **scroll-scrubbed** section — the showpiece sequence.

<Callout type="tip" title="Don't reach for animation first">
  The best animation is often a CSS `transition` on a single property. Bring in Motion or GSAP when
  you need coordinated states, sequencing, gestures, layout, or scroll — not for a one-property
  hover. Libraries are for the animations CSS can't express cleanly.
</Callout>

## A quick rule of thumb

- Animating **because a component's state/props changed**, or you want **gestures/layout/exit**? → **Motion**.
- Building a **timeline**, need **precise sequencing**, or tying motion to **scroll**? → **GSAP**.
- Not sure and it's a single property? → a **CSS transition**.

## Recap

- **Motion** = declarative, React-lifecycle, gestures, layout, exit.
- **GSAP** = imperative, timelines, precise sequencing, scroll (ScrollTrigger).
- **Use both** where each fits; reach for neither when a CSS transition will do.
