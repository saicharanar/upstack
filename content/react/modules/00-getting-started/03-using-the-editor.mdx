Every graded chapter ends with a hands-on **workspace** that runs real React right in your
browser — nothing to install, no setup. This short chapter shows you how it's laid out so the
exercises feel comfortable from the first one.

## The three panels

When you launch an exercise, you get three panels working together:

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
    E["Editor — you write code"] -->|"as you type"| P["Live preview — your component runs"]
    E -->|"run the checks"| T["Automated checks — one per requirement"]
    T -->|"all pass"| D["Chapter complete"]`}
  caption="Write in the editor, see it live in the preview, and let the checks verify each requirement."
/>

- **Editor** — where you write your code, with syntax highlighting. It's a real editor; type
  as you would anywhere.
- **Live preview** — your component, rendered and running. It updates as you type, so you can
  *see* what you're building, not just imagine it.
- **Automated checks** — a list of tests, each verifying one requirement. Green means that
  requirement passes; red tells you exactly what's still off.

<Callout type="tip" title="Examples are runnable too">
  The blue "Runnable example" boxes inside chapters work the same way — edit the code and
  watch the preview change. Poke at them; there's a **⤢ Full screen** button in the corner
  when an example needs more room.
</Callout>

## How a chapter becomes complete

There are two ways a chapter turns to ✓ **complete**:

1. **Pass its exercise.** When every required check goes green, the chapter is marked complete
   and the concepts you demonstrated are recorded.
2. **Mark it by hand.** Concept chapters like this one have no exercise — read them and click
   **Mark chapter as complete**. You can always reverse it with **Mark as not done**.

## Nothing is locked; your progress is saved

<Callout type="note" title="Learn in any order">
  No chapter is gated behind another — jump around freely. Your progress (which chapters are
  done, which is in progress, and where you left off) is saved locally in your browser, and the
  **Resume** button on the home page always brings you back to where you stopped.
</Callout>

## Reading the checks

The checks are written in plain language — "renders the avatar image with the correct alt
text", not internal jargon. Each maps to a concept the chapter teaches, so a red check points
you straight at the idea to revisit. (You'll even learn *why* they're written that way in the
final chapter on testing.)

That's the whole loop: **read, build, run the checks, move on.** Mark this complete and let's
write your first component.
